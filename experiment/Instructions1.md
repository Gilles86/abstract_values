Your task will be to learn the relation between the angle of a gabor (angled black and white colored lines on a circle - Figure 1 shows you an example) and its monetary value. Each gabor has a specific monetary value assigned to it according to its angle (like a bank note). To learn the relation, you will first be shown all possible angles of the gabor together with their corresponding monetary value (Phase 1). After that you will be shown a random gabor and you need to input the monetary amount that matches its value. You will get feedback on this (Phase 2). Finally, you will again be shown a gabor and can bid money to purchase it. Later, you can sell it for its actual monetary value that you learned in the previous phase (Phase 3). Since your bid is the maximum price you will have to pay to buy the gabor (the price is randomly determined, up to your bid), you are best off to bid the real monetary value of the gabor. Note that in Phase 3, there is no feedback.  So, in the end, you will be paid according to how well you learned the relationship between the gabors and their values in phase 2, and according to the difference between the random price set by the computer if you won the bid and the resell value of the gabors you purchased in phase 3.

Second phase of the experiment

In the second phase of the experiment, you will be shown gabor and have to input their associated monetary value. The gabor will be shown in the center of your screen. After 1.5 seconds, you will also see a bar for the input (see Figure 4). You can move the slider on the bar using the mouse. When you click the mouse you confirm your estimate.

For each round, your task is to adjust the slider to match the monetary value of the gabor’s angle, as you learned in Phase 1. Use your mouse to move the slider along the bar until you reach the value you believe corresponds to the gabor’s angle. To confirm your choice, click the mouse button. You will then receive feedback: if your input exactly matches the gabor’s true value, the feedback will be green; if not, it will be red, and the correct value will be displayed at the top of the gabor. For every gabor, ask yourself: What is the monetary value of this gabor? In other words, how much money would I get if I sold this gabor? You have 5 seconds to make your guess. This phase consists of 230 rounds, with each of the 23 unique gabors appearing 10 times in a random order.
Try to predict the monetary value of the gabors in this phase as accurately as possible. Your payment will depend on how well you have learned this relationship. You will earn more if you are able to guess the associated value of the gabor with the least possible difference to the true value in each guess.


Third and final phase of the experiment
You can take short breaks between blocks. Take a moment to rest if you need to, but always return promptly to continue the experiment.
In the third and final phase of the experiment, you have to input the monetary value of a presented (angled) gabor as a BID in an AUCTION (see Figure 5). The difference to the previous phase is that you will not receive feedback and that you only have a limited amount of time to input a value (4 seconds). The gabor will also be shown with less contrast, so it will be harder to see the image. The value of the gabor is exactly what you learnt in the first two phases of the experiment. 


Your budget for each trial is 42 CHF. You will keep the rest of the budget after paying the price of the gabor if you win the bidding. The gabors you brought will be exchanged for money at the end of the experiment, and you will get 1/184 of the money you win on every trial here as extra payment (so the average earnings over all trial). As you will see in a moment, your best strategy is to bid your true value for the gabor.

To submit your bid, click the mouse button to confirm the slider’s position. If you do not confirm your bid by clicking, your input will not be registered, and you will not receive any payout for that trial. You have 4.5 seconds to make your decision, so always submit a bid within the time limit to maximize your earnings.
In every auction there is a rule that determines the price at which the items are sold. This auction is no different. 

The rule for our auction is a bit unusual, but its implications are straightforward: There is no way of gaming the auction, the BEST thing that you can do in every trial is to ask yourself how much you would be willing to pay to get THAT gabor and then bid the number closest to that value. 

What is the rule? At the end of each trial, the computer will choose a random price for the gabor which will range between 2 and 42 (the range of possible values of the gabor). If your bid is greater than the random price, you will get the gabor for the random price. You can then sell it in the end for its true value and earn the difference between the true value and the random price you paid for it. If your bid is less or equal than the random value, you will NOT get the gabor but will not have to pay any money and won’t earn any additional money from this trial. 

Why is it in your interest to bid the value closest to your true value for the gabor? 

You might think that your best strategy is to bid less than the gabor is worth to you. This is INCORRECT. The payout you get is determined by the random price that the computer draws (your earnings are the difference between the true value of the gabor and the random price you paid) and NOT by your bid. Thus, if you lower your bid you would not be able to affect the payout, but might end up losing the opportunity to get the gabor at a “good” price (and therefore not earn any additional payout). 

For example, suppose that the gabor is worth 10 CHF to you. If you bid your true value, you will get the gabor if the random value is anything between 2 and 10 CHF. You will not get the gabor if the random value is 10 CHF or above. It follows that by bidding your true value you make a “profit” since you always end up paying less than the gabor is worth to you. 

Clearly, you should also never bid more than your true value. (If you bid more than your true value and you win the bid for a price higher than the true value, your payout will be negative.)

What happens if you bid less than you true value? For example, suppose that you bid 2 CHF even though your true value is 10 CHF. In this case you never get the gabor, which is not a good strategy since, when you bid your true value, you pretty much always end up paying less than the gabor is worth to you! 

Note another attractive property of the auction rules: conditional on getting the gabor (i.e, conditional on the random number being less or equal to your bid), the price that you pay is on average half of your bid. 

Thus, if you follow your best strategy and always bid the true value, on a typical trial you will get to buy the gabor at approximately half its true value (and thus earn half its value as the corresponding payout)! 

Don’t let the details of the experiment confuse you, phase 3 trials are actually very simple: 
You will make bids for purchasing a gabor on 184 trials.
You should treat each decision as if it were the only one. 
Your best strategy is to always bid the number closest to your true value for getting that gabor at the end of the experiment.  
If you follow the best strategy, on average the price that you pay for a gabor will be half of the value you bid.
In the end, you will get 1/184 of the total money you accumulated during the trials you participated in during this phase as real money.
